## Round 1 — 0.80 (piano tempering)

Theo wanted the thirds pure. He'd said it twice while Marisol laid out her felt strip and rubber mutes, said it the way people repeat what they've rehearsed: the file was due to the conservatory by midnight, the panel would hear his daughter play the Raindrop in D-flat and nothing else. He could feel the beating in a tempered third, he said, the way you feel a coin through a mattress. He had the ears for it; she could tell from how he hummed the leading tone a hair flat to test her. Junie sat on the stairs with her knees up, eleven. Marisol seated her lever on the D-flat and listened to the third — ten flutters a second, sharp, the even smear that lets a single piano play in all twelve keys, each off by the same hair. She could still those flutters. Pure thirds in D-flat, a chord like flat water. It would also sour the keys Junie practiced every morning, the C and the F of her Czerny, for the four months until anyone called her back.

She tuned it even. She brought every third up to the same tempered sharpness, the D-flat he loved along with the rest, counting the beats back into the home key on purpose, her eyes down. When she finished she told him she'd taken the home key as close to pure as the instrument would hold without losing the others. He sat and played the opening and his face loosened; he heard sweetness because he wanted it, because a phone would flatten the difference to nothing he'd hear. Junie caught it. She had come down off the stairs and stood at the bass end through the whole tuning, watching the hands, and while her father bent over the keys she pressed the F and the C a fifth above it, held them down, and looked at Marisol over the ring of it, not smiling.

## Round 2 — 0.80 (queen-bee wing-clipping)

Mara held the flashlight the way she had at nine, the beam wandering off into the almond rows where the bloom stood closed and waiting on these forty boxes to crack it open. Two hundred dollars a colony if the bees worked it, nothing if a box absconded in the night. Del had the queen up in the marking cage between her finger and thumb, half again as long as any worker on the frame and the whole trouble besides: too big to slip the excluder she'd laid at dusk, the wires set a hair over four millimeters, sized so the small bees could thread up to the honey and the queen could not. The scissors were in her coat. A third of one forewing and the queen would never fly a swarm out of here; she'd tumble into the grass and the cloud would pour back down to find her. Bees don't feel the cut. Del had done a dozen by headlamp that week. "I'm not signing on for next February," Mara said. "I wanted you to hear it from me, not off the schedule."

Del turned the cage so the queen walked the screen, that heavy unhurried walk, both forewings whole and catching the headlamp. The bloom would open in two days whether this one stayed or not. She could pin the wing and keep the box. The truck would be loaded before the sun cleared the Sierra, and Mara would drive the first leg the way she always had. Del thumbed the cage open over the frame and let the queen down uncut into the dark between the combs, and the workers closed over her. Then she held the flashlight out to her daughter. "Keep it steady on the tailgate," she said. "We've still got the far pallets to strap."

## Round 3 — 0.78 (piano inharmonicity + hearing loss)

Petra slid her felt strips between the strings and listened to the top octave the way Walter had taught her to mistrust it. A piano's high notes are tuned a little sharp on purpose. The strings are stiff, their overtones run ahead of the arithmetic, and a tuner chases that error upward so the octaves ring instead of lying there dead. Walter knew the trick better than she did; he'd shown it to her years ago on this same upright. Forty years on a brake press had since taken the top of his hearing and left something jagged where it used to be. The highest A reached him doubled now, two pitches a hair apart, a wasp sealed in a jar. It was the note he had always called the prettiest in the house. He'd told her last month at the door, low, while Iris was in the kitchen with the tap running. Bring it down, he said. Just the top octave. I can't stand it true anymore. Iris still had the ears she was born with, and she had spent a lifetime hearing that octave the bright way it was built to sound.

Stretched, the octave sang for Iris and stung him. Pulled down to where his ruined ear would forgive it, it would go flat and patient under Iris's hands, and any tuner she called afterward would read the slackened octave as Petra's mistake, and Iris would feel the brightness leave the room tonight without being given a reason. Petra flattened the octave. She worked slowly, listening the beats down one by one, and when she struck the A at last it settled low and a little tired. Walter's shoulders eased. In the doorway Iris went still, a cup in each hand, watching the back of his head. Petra wound the felt back onto its spool and did not look up. Behind her Walter drew out the bench and sat, and the first chord came soft and even, and Iris stayed where she was.

## Round 4 — 0.80 (avalanche control)

Lund's knee told him about the cold before the thermometer did. He stood at the top of Cirque with the radio warm against his chest and watched Marquez load the last hand charge, the kid's gloves quick on the fuse igniter, too quick. Below them the start zone lay unmarked under a foot of Tuesday's storm, and eight inches down under that, the layer Lund didn't trust: surface hoar from the clear week in January, buried now, feathered crystals bonded to nothing. They'd thrown two shots into it already. Both cracked flat and loud. The slope held. A slope that takes a bomb and keeps its feet is a slope waiting for something with a heartbeat. The route ended here. Past this the resort wanted the rope down by eight, and the first-chair crowd was already stacking at the maze, their breath rising over the corral.

For nine seasons the cut had been his. You drop into the start zone on a downhill traverse and feel for the give, the whole hill settling an inch under you with a sound like a door shutting in another house, the whumpf. Today Marquez wanted it. He didn't ask so much as stand at the rollover, pole planted, looking back. The slow column of Lund's March traverse times was posted in the patrol shack for anyone to read. He could take the cut anyway. His knee would hold for one. Instead he keyed the radio and read Marquez onto the slope by name. He watched the kid go. He felt no give under his own boots, because his boots were not on the snow; the release happened forty yards out, where the kid's line crossed the buried hoar and the apron sat down all at once with a crack that reached him a half second after he saw it move. Marquez rode it onto the rib, whooping. Lund unclipped from the anchor and crouched to coil the long line into his pack, hand over elbow. The radio asked him a second time whether Cirque was open.

## Round 6 — 0.81 (borosilicate annealing)

The strain viewer showed it before Marisol wanted to see it: a thumb-smear of green and magenta along the seam where the manifold's side-arm met the body, color where clear glass holds none. She had brought it down too fast. You cool borosilicate slow, five hundred and sixty degrees and then an hour of nothing, or the stress stays sealed inside for days, a year, until vacuum or heat finds it. Dewa's thesis ran at seven that morning, her whole committee leaning in, flammable solvent drawn through this exact glass under a vacuum where a hidden crack would implode and throw burning solvent across the bench. The piece looked perfect. Teo stood in the doorway holding both their coats, the truck already running in the lot, their mother's table set for eight and his flight gone by noon. "It'll hold for one run," he said. The certificate on the wall outweighed one grad student's morning, and if the manifold let go with Dewa bent over it the shop's name went on the report and no lab in the state would buy a paid inch of Marisol's glass again.

She heard all of it and reached past him to bring the kiln up to annealing heat. She would build the joint over again and sit with it through the slow cool. The table would be set for seven. Teo hung her coat back on the hook by the door and left with his own. The truck idled in the lot, then pulled away. She fed a cold rod into the flame, waited for the tip to slump soft against the broken seam, her thumb blistering where she held it too near the heat, and kept it there.

## Round 9 — 0.81 (orchard frost protection)

Marin had been awake since the dew point crossed thirty-four. Her father stood at the end of the equipment shed with his coat open, not helping. Helping was hers now; they had settled that in March without quite saying so. The post thermometer read thirty-six and falling. The stars were out hard and close, the way they came only on the clear nights that did the killing, and she made herself walk the arithmetic one more time the way he had drilled it into her. The pond would throw a tenth of an inch an hour. A tenth of an inch would hold the buds at freezing till morning, because water gives off heat as it sets; every film of ice that grew on a blossom bled a little warmth back into it. But the pond would carry one block, not two. Spread across both, the rate dropped under the line, and then the ice took more heat by evaporation than it gave and you lost everything. One block lived. She had known that since dinner. What she had not done was touch the valve.

The old Spies stood up the rise, forty trees her father had grafted himself. Cold air drains downhill. They sat above the worst of it and might, on their own, take a light frost and hold. The Honeycrisp had the low ground and the bank note both. At a quarter to one she opened the lower line. The heads began their sweep. She walked up through the dark and shut the gate to the Spies while there was still nothing wrong with them. Her father watched her do it and said nothing. By four the Honeycrisp stood sheathed and glittering under the yard light, every twig swollen with ice and ticking, alive inside its own glass. The Spies took the frost bare. When the sun cleared the ridge Marin climbed the rise. She pulled a spur down and split a bud with her thumbnail; where it should have shown green it was the soft brown of bruised fruit. Her father came up the row behind her, slower than he used to be, and put out his hand for the next one.
